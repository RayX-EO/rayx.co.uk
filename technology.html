Technology

Physics in, crop intelligence out.

RayX is built on radiative transfer theory — the mathematics of how light interacts with the atmosphere, plant canopies, and soil. This page explains what that means in practice and why it matters for the quality of the outputs.

Foundation

Why physics-based, not data-driven?

Many remote sensing products rely on machine learning trained on historical correlations. RayX takes a different approach.

Transferability

Physics works in places without training data.

A radiative transfer model describes how light behaves. It doesn't need to be retrained for a new region, crop, or sensor. This means RayX can work in areas where labelled datasets are sparse or nonexistent.

Interpretability

Every output has a physical meaning.

When ARC estimates a leaf area index of 3.2, that number corresponds to a real physical quantity. It's not an abstract feature learned by a neural network — it's a measurement you can validate and explain.

Consistency

The model doesn't drift with the data.

Statistical models can degrade when the input distribution shifts — a new sensor, an unusual season, a different crop mix. Physics-based models handle these changes because they describe the underlying process, not just the pattern.

Layer roles

Each layer solves a different problem.

The value of the chain is that each layer improves the next. But each one also solves a standalone problem.

S

SIAC: Atmospheric correction

From top-of-atmosphere to surface reflectance

Models the atmosphere's effect on satellite observations and removes it. Uses radiative transfer physics to estimate aerosol optical depth, water vapour, and ozone, then inverts the atmospheric contribution to recover what the surface actually reflects.

A

ARC: Crop state modelling

From surface reflectance to biophysical parameters

Models how light interacts with plant canopies — leaf tissue, canopy geometry, soil background — and inverts multi-spectral observations to recover the crop properties that best explain the signal. Outputs include leaf area index, canopy cover, chlorophyll, and growth stage.

Y

Yield: Production forecasting

From crop parameters to yield signals

Translates the biophysical crop trajectory from ARC into production estimates, anomaly detection, and risk signals. When the forecast changes, you can trace the change back to specific crop parameters in specific areas — the evidence chain stays visible.
